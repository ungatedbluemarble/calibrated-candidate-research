# Calibrated Candidate Research

What the newest research on AI hiring actually proves, and the few moves it shows make a difference for the people sending out resumes.

**By Mathew Baldwin, [@ungatedbluemarble](https://github.com/ungatedbluemarble)**

---

## What this is

Roughly nine in ten U.S. employers now screen job applicants with AI, most of it bought from the same small handful of vendors. This repository holds two pieces that work through what that concentration does to job seekers and where a candidate has real, evidence-backed room to respond.

The first piece is a short, plain-language read on what the research means for anyone job hunting in 2026. The second is a technical and compliance analysis: the source-exact figures from each study held to their specific metrics, the regulatory frame around Title VII, NYC Local Law 144, and the EU AI Act, and a disciplined account of where a candidate-side toolkit can legitimately help and where it cannot.

Both pieces are grounded in two studies: "Algorithmic Monocultures in Hiring" (Bommasani, Bana, Creel, Jurafsky, and Liang, FAccT 2026), the largest deployment-scale study of hiring algorithms to date, and "AI Self-preferencing in Algorithmic Hiring" (Xu, Li, and Jiang, EAAMO 2025), a controlled experiment on how large language models evaluate resumes. The argument is deliberately bounded: it claims the mechanism the studies prove, not magnitudes the studies do not transfer, and it states plainly that a candidate-side tool improves how an applicant meets the screening layer rather than whether they get hired.
